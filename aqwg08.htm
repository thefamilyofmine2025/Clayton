BIOGRAPHY: When my father was seven, his mother died of encephalitis. It is most likely his mother Bessie, who was a talented singer who played guitar (or ukelele), from which dad inherited his musical ability.

BIOGRAPHY: His grandfather, John M. Clayton, was a country doctor who served West Louisville for more than 60 years.  As a boy, my father would travel along on his calls and run errands, such as bringing water and towels to the mother giving birth or sometimes just explaining what the Doc needed from the family. When asked if what he witnessed scared or disgusted him, he says no, he felt happy to be able to help out. It helps explain my dad?s mellow nature. He had a strong role model.

BIOGRAPHY: He remembers Granddad Clayton fondly, remembering how, after his eyesight started to weaken, he'd continually backing into the same (cherry?) tree along the driveway and of the many mailboxes that fell victim in his haste to get to patients.

BIOGRAPHY: When dads father was still living, Momma Dear would plunk him and sister Maggie down at the corner opposite the dairy company on Brook Street to wait for dad to leave at the end of the day. One day while they were sitting down, they decided kill time by taking Mickie and Tinka, the family's German Shepard police dogs, for a quick walk around the block. As they were heading back, they heard a terrific explosion. A car had careened out of control at the intersection and had slammed into the brick wall they normally rested against with such force it knocked it over completely.

BIOGRAPHY:  One passion he must have inherited from the wise country doctor, who won a general practitioner of the year award in 1959, was his love of politics. He describes the doctors office being full of stacks of Pathfinder, an early socialist publication. Granddad Clayton, along with his sons Stirman and Charlie, were all strong Democrats. When someone would visit, they would get more than just medical advice as Dr. Clayton doled out political insights, shuffling around the room until he could locate the particular issue he was referring to.

BIOGRAPHY: My fathers generosity which extends to all aspects of his life also must have its roots with Dr. Clayton.

BIOGRAPHY: Not even counting all the money and gifts my father and mother have doled out to their children over the years, I can't tell you how many times I've seen my father hand someone a book off his shelf or a tennis racquet to try out, etc. For years he ordered beautiful Rand McNally road atlases by the dozens just to hand out to friends who stopped by. His giving nature even extends to dining out, where he nudges everyone at the table to get a taste of his dessert.

BIOGRAPHY: When Dr. Clayton died, a friend (or Stirman) went over his books and discovered that he was a millionaire on paper. But most of the West Louisville farmers could only pay bills with what they grew or tended, so that's what Dr. Clayton accepted.

BIOGRAPHY: John M. and Maggie attended Mount St. Joseph Academy Maple Mount, Ky, where their mother had also gone. It was a womens college also, had a beautiful campus, with an elementary school enrolling mostly farm children taught by Nuns. Also took in homeless. Mary Ann Lindeman, parents had died. In his class of about 45 children, John M and Maggie were top students who competed with each other. When he returned many years later with Maggie, one of the nuns still remembered them, and dad's nickname Skippy.

BIOGRAPHY:          His mother was very devout, when the Catholic Church still said mass in Latin. Dad had the job of pumping the organ. During mass when Priest would sing a phrase, the brand new organ was supposed to answer them. Dad and his little friends would be talking about marbles, distracted and forget to pump it. Organist would give him dagger eyes.

BIOGRAPHY:          He remembers the night before his first communion, his mother catching him dipping the drinking spoon in the water on the stove (no running water at that time). She screamed no! but it was too late, so I had to take communion by myself a day later. I thought it was quite a treat.

BIOGRAPHY: He started his first year of high school in West Louisville Kentucky. Remembers his first clarinet solo in front of the school recital, scared to death. One of his baseball teammates kidded him his solo was so low he could hardly hear it.  After he became ill Auntie Stella convinced his grandparents to sign over custody of dad.
