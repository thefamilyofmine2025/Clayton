Francis Lemuel Clayton, proprietor of Lem Clayton's sample rooms and billiard parlors, was born near Bardwtown, Nelson County, Ky, Nov 3, 1845, and is a son of John and Maria (Hayden) Clayton, natives of Ky. They had a family of four sons and seven daughters, Francis L being the youngest. He was reared on his father's farm, attending school in the winter until he turned 15. On September 25, 1861 he enlisted in Company G, 10th Kentucky Infantry as a drummer boy. He was discharged after twenty-one months and five days because of disability. The next two years he farmed. Next he opened a saloon at Loretto, Marion County, Kentucky, where his Uncle William was living. After two years he moved to Daviess County, Kentucky and clerked for Henry Reimart's interest and in March 1881 opened Lem Clayton's Sample roon and Billiard Parlor. It was the finest business of its type in the city. Mr Clayton married Anna Simms, Sept 10, 1874. She was born in Marion County, Kentucky, and is a daughter of Milburn Sims. Mr and Mrs Clayton had four children - - John M, Charles L, William C, and James H.

Mr Clayton married his present wife, Jessie Cummings, Jan 14, 1877. She was born in Owensboro and is a daughter of William Cummings, who was in the livery business for a number of years.  Mr and Mrs Clayton have had three children, one living - Mabel. The other two died in infancy. Mr Clayton is a member of the G.A.R. (Grand Army of the Republic)  In politics he is a Republican. (History of Daviess County - Curdsville Precinct - pg 444)

In the latter part of his life he took the Keeley cure for alcoholism and sent a card to the newspaper praising the Keeley Institute and exclaiming the merits of its curative powers.

Lem was operated on for a leg tumor and the leg was amputated at the knee. When Francis Lemuel Clayton died in 1897, his obituary described his life of professional gambling, poker being his forte. He was described as completely honest and not an enemy in the world. He had quietly  given away a vast amount of money to needy individuals.

His funeral was held at St Stephens Church in Owensboro, Kentucky. Owensboro Examiner  July 1877.

BIGAMY
Mr. Lem Clayton was arrested and tried before Judge Triplette last Wednesday under a warrent charging him with bigamy. Clayton produced a divorce granted by the authorities of Utah, and objected to the jurisdiction of the court. After a lengthy argument by the attorneys, the court ruled that he had jurisdiction and proceeded to hear the case and held Clayton over in a bond of $300 dollars for his appearance at the next September term of the Daviess Criminal Court. A large crowd was present and considerable interest was manifested. The Commonwealth was ably represented by W. T. Ellis, county attorney, and the defense by Hon. Wm, N. Sweeney.
